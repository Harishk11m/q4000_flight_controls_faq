Flight Controls Q&A

General Flight Control System

1. What are the subsystems of the Flight Control System?

The subsystems are the Flight Control Electronic Control Unit (FCECU), Aileron Control System, Rudder Control System, Elevator Control System, Wing Flaps, Spoilers Control System, and Gust Lock Systems.

2. How are the primary flight control surfaces operated?

The primary flight control surfaces are operated by cables from the left or right control columns and rudder pedals, which allows the aircraft to be flown from either the pilot or copilot positions.

3. Where are the positions of the rudder, roll spoilers, and elevators displayed?

The positions of the rudder, roll spoilers, and elevators are displayed on the Powered Flight Control Surfaces Indicator (PFCSI) on the pilot's Multi-Functional Display.

4. What is the purpose of the Flight Control Electronic Control Unit (FCECU)?

The FCECU receives electrical/electronic inputs and supplies electrical/electronic outputs for all primary and secondary flight control systems. It is a single unit divided into two separate channels, left and right, that receive inputs from the pilot and/or copilot flight commands.

5. What are some of the functions that the FCECU is part of?

The FCECU is part of several functions, including Rudder Hinge Moment Limiting (HML), Pitch Trim, Elevator Feel, Spoiler Lift-Dump, and Flight Control Surface position reporting to the Flight Data Recorder (FDR) and the Powered Flight Control Surface Indicator (PFCSI).

Aileron Control System

1. How are roll commands transmitted from the pilot's and copilot's control handwheels to the aileron surfaces?

Roll commands are transmitted through a series of quadrants, cables, levers, and pushrods.

2. What is the purpose of the Automatic Flight Control System (AFCS) in the aileron system?

The AFCS provides roll commands through an autopilot servo connected to the aileron forward quadrant.

3. How is aileron trim achieved, and what unit is involved?

Aileron trim is achieved by electrically trimming both aileron surfaces through an actuator in the Aileron Trim and Centering Unit.

4. Where are the two aileron control surfaces located on the aircraft?

There is one aileron surface on the outboard end of each wing, and they are part of the trailing edge of their respective wings.

5. What is the purpose of the geared tab on each aileron?

The geared tab moves in the opposite direction of the aileron surface to provide aerodynamic assistance, which reduces the force required to turn the pilot's and copilot's handwheels.

6. What functions does the FCECU perform for the aileron control system?

The FCECU supplies electrical excitation for the Linear Variable Differential Transducer (LVDT) on the right aileron, and it receives aileron position data from the LVDT to transmit to the Flight Data Recorder (FDR).

Rudder Control System

1. How are yaw commands transmitted to the rudder?

The rudder system transmits yaw commands from the pilot's and copilot's rudder pedals to the rudder through a series of quadrants, cables, levers, and pushrods. This movement is transmitted to two hydraulic Power Control Units (PCUs) that move the rudder in proportion to the pedal input.

2. What is the purpose of the rudder feel and trim unit?

The rudder feel and trim unit provides artificial feel to the rudder pedals and is used to prevent the pilot from over-controlling the aircraft during flight.

3. How does rudder trimming work?

Rudder trimming, controlled by the trim actuator, biases the complete rudder system so that a newly trimmed position is sensed by the pilot as the neutral position.

4. What is the purpose of Rudder Hinge Moment Limiting (HML) as performed by the FCECU?

The FCECU limits the rudder hinge moment by regulating the pressure in the rudder PCU based on aircraft speed. It decreases the regulated PCU pressure as airspeed increases to reduce total rudder travel at higher speeds.

5. How does the FCECU monitor for failures in the rudder PCU's regulated pressure?

The FCECU receives pressure signals from two pressure sensors located in each PCU, which allows it to monitor the regulated PCU pressure for any failure modes.

6. What happens if a rudder PCU fails or is faulty?

The FCECU will depressurize a faulty PCU, and if one PCU fails, it will increase the pressure to the operating PCU. It also sends signals to the rudder caution lights and pushoff switchlights to indicate system malfunctions.

7. What happens if a pressure failure occurs in a rudder PCU?

If a pressure failure occurs, the FCECU commands the rudder PCU shutoff valve to depressurize the PCU, and the corresponding RUD 1 HYD or RUD 2 HYD caution light will come on.

8. What caution lights does the FCECU turn on related to the rudder system?

The FCECU sends signals to turn on the #1 RUD HYD, #2 RUD HYD, and RUD CNTRL caution lights.

9. What does the FCECU do if both No. 1 and No. 2 hydraulic systems fail?

If both hydraulic systems fail, the FCECU will cause the RUD CNTRL caution light to come on.

Elevator Control System

1. What is the function of the elevator system?

The elevator system transmits pitch commands from the pilot's and copilot's control columns to the elevator control surfaces, which then move in proportion to the control column movement to pitch the aircraft nose up or down.

2. What is the purpose of trimming the elevator control system?

Trimming the elevator control system provides the aircraft with longitudinal stability during flight and counters the effects of changes to the aircraft's center of gravity, airspeed, and lift during flap extension or retraction.

3. Where are the elevator surfaces located, and how many are there?

There are two elevator surfaces, left and right, located on the trailing edge of the left and right horizontal stabilizers.

4. What is the purpose of the Stall Warning / Stick Pusher system?

The system provides the flight crew with warnings of an impending stall and can push the control columns forward to assist with stall recovery.

5. How does the Stall Warning / Stick Pusher system warn the crew of an impending stall?

Warnings of an impending stall are provided by column stick shakers and an audio warning.

6. How does the FCECU influence the elevator feel and pitch trim?

The FCECU changes the elevator feel forces and manual pitch trim rate based on airspeed and acceleration. It also supplies automatic pitch trim when the autopilot is engaged or when the flaps are extended or retracted.

7. What warnings and monitoring does the FCECU provide for the elevator system?

The FCECU monitors for pitch trim actuator failures, elevator asymmetry, and elevator feel actuator failures, and it supplies signals to the respective caution lights. It also provides an aural warning on takeoff if the elevator trim is outside the takeoff range.

Spoiler Control System

1. What is the primary function of the roll spoilers system?

The roll spoilers are a secondary, hydraulically powered flight control system that works with the ailerons to provide lateral control of the aircraft during flight. They also help decrease the lift created by the wings during landing.

2. How do the ailerons and roll spoilers operate together to provide lateral control?

Rotary movement of either the pilot's or copilot's control handwheels operates both the ailerons and spoilers at the same time through an interconnect rod that joins the two control columns.

3. What are the three modes of spoiler operation?

The three modes of spoiler operation are Flight Mode, Ground Mode, and Taxi Mode.

4. What happens if a jam occurs in either the aileron or roll spoiler system?

The ailerons and roll spoilers can be disconnected from each other, allowing the aircraft to be operated with limited roll control using the unjammed system.

5. How many spoiler panels are there and where are they located?

There are four spoiler panels, with an inboard and an outboard panel on each wing. They are installed on the upper surface of the wing forward of the outboard flaps.

6. What hydraulic systems supply pressure to the spoiler panels?

The Power Control Units (PCUs) for the inboard spoiler panels are supplied by the No. 1 hydraulic system, while the PCUs for the outboard spoiler panels are supplied by the No. 2 hydraulic system.

7. What is the maximum angle to which the spoiler panels can extend?

The spoiler panels can extend upwards to a maximum of 75 degrees when actuated.

8. What happens to the spoilers in Flight Mode at airspeeds greater than 170 kt (315 km/h)?

At airspeeds greater than 170 kt, the FCECU locks out the outboard spoilers by depressurizing their Power Control Units (PCUs). The inboard spoilers remain operational at all airspeeds.

9. What happens when a clutch slips in the spoiler system?

When a clutch slips due to a PCU linkage jam, the applicable spoiler advisory switchlight (SPLR1 or SPLR 2 PUSH OFF) comes on. Pushing the switchlight depressurizes the applicable spoiler PCUs, causing them to retract to the 0-degree position.

10. What happens when a hydraulic system fails for the spoilers?

If the No. 1 hydraulic system fails, the ROLL SPLR INBD HYD caution light comes on. If the No. 2 hydraulic system fails, the ROLL SPLR OUTBD HYD caution light comes on.

Ground Spoiler System

1. What is the main purpose of the ground spoilers system?

The ground spoilers are deployed symmetrically to "dump" the lift created by the wings. This puts all of the aircraft's weight on the landing gear, making the wheel brakes more effective for slowing the aircraft.

2. Under what conditions do the spoilers enter Ground Mode?

Ground Mode operation occurs when the Proximity Sensor Electronic Unit (PSEU) gives the Weight-On-Wheels (WOW) signal to the Flight Control Electronic Control Unit (FCECU), power levers 1 and 2 are set to less than 12 degrees above flight idle, and the spoiler FLIGHT/TAXI switch is in the FLIGHT position.

3. What happens when the ground spoilers are deployed?

When the ground spoilers are extended, the ROLL INBD and ROLL OUTBD advisory lights come on. The wing lift is "dumped," which increases the weight on the landing gear to give maximum braking efficiency.

4. What happens if a lift dump solenoid valve fails to energize after landing?

If a solenoid valve fails to energize, the inboard or outboard ground spoilers will not extend. The applicable ROLL SPLR INBD GND or ROLL SPLR OUTBD GND caution light will come on after a time delay of 5 seconds.

5. What happens if a pilot needs to perform a go-around after the ground spoilers have been deployed?

If a go-around is necessary, the pilot advances the power levers. This removes power to the lift dump solenoid valves and the roll advisory lights. All spoiler panels will retract, and the roll advisory lights will go off.

6. Why are there two lift dump valves for each spoiler system connected in series?

The two valves in each system are connected hydraulically in series. Both valves must open to let pressure through to the related spoiler PCU. This reduces the possibility of accidental in-flight symmetric deployment.

Flap Control System

1. What is the purpose of the wing flaps?

The wing flaps provide increased lift during take-off and landing. Each wing has an inboard and an outboard flap surface.

2. How are the flaps controlled and what are the possible flap positions?

The flaps are electronically controlled and hydraulically operated. The flap quadrant on the center console has five positions corresponding to the five possible flap positions: 0°, 5°, 10°, 15°, and 35°.

3. What is the function of the flap control system?

The flap control system is an electronically controlled, hydro-mechanical system that uses a digital position control loop to operate the flap surfaces. It monitors flap position and selection signals and produces output signals to the Flap Power Unit (FPU) to control flap movement.

4. What are the two functions of the flap position indication system?

The two functions are to provide an indication of the current flap position to the copilot's Multi-Function Display and to monitor the position of the flaps during deployment and send data to the Flap Control Unit (FCU).

Gust Lock Systems

1. What is the purpose of the gust lock system?

The gust lock system locks the aileron control surfaces in the neutral position to protect them from damage. It is operated from the flight compartment.

2. Where is the aileron gust lock control lever located?

The CONTROL LOCK lever is located in the flight compartment on the power quadrant.

3. What happens when the CONTROL LOCK lever is moved to the ON position?

When the lever is moved to the ON position, the aileron splitter quadrant is locked in the neutral position, the ailerons are locked in the neutral position, and the power levers cannot be advanced.

4. What happens if a gust lock cable fails?

If a gust lock cable fails, the gust lock mechanism fails safe to the unlocked position.

5. What happens if the ailerons are not in the neutral position when the lever is moved to ON?

If the ailerons are not in the neutral position, the plunger is compressed against the face of the lock plate. The plunger springs out to the locked position when the ailerons are moved to neutral.

Disconnect Systems

1. What is the function of the roll disconnect system?

The roll disconnect system disengages the aileron and roll spoiler control circuits from each other if a jam occurs in either circuit. The remaining operational circuit maintains roll (lateral) control of the aircraft.

2. How is the roll disconnect handle activated?

The roll disconnect handle is pulled to its limit and turned 90 degrees to separate the clutch plate and lever assembly from the clutch and sprocket assembly. This allows the control handwheels to rotate independently of each other.

3. How is the clutch re-engaged after it has been disconnected?

To re-engage the clutch, the handle must be rotated 90 degrees and released. The handle and clutch mechanism will return to the engaged position when the two control wheels come into alignment.

4. What is the function of the pitch disconnect system?

The pitch disconnect system allows the left and right elevator control circuits to be disconnected from each other if a jam occurs in either circuit. The remaining operational circuit can then control the aircraft's pitch.

5. How is the pitch disconnect system activated?

If a jam occurs, the PITCH DISC handle in the flight compartment is pulled to disconnect the left elevator circuit from the right elevator circuit, giving pitch control to the pilot with the free control column.

FCECU Interfaces and Warnings

1. What parameter does the FCECU receive from the Air Data Units (ADUs)?

The FCECU receives airspeed data from the ADUs.

2. What signal does the FCECU receive from the Power Lever Angle Microswitches?

The FCECU receives the FLIGHT IDLE +12° signal from the Power Lever Angle Microswitches.

3. What signals does the FCECU receive from the Pitch Disconnect Relay?

The FCECU receives an engaged/disengaged signal from the Pitch Disconnect Relay.

4. What are some of the caution lights that the FCECU can turn on?

The FCECU can turn on several caution lights, including ELEVATOR FEEL, PITCH TRIM, ELEVATOR ASYMMETRY, and various lights for rudder and spoiler hydraulic and ground conditions.

5. Which pushoff switchlights does the FCECU send signals to?

The FCECU sends signals to the RUD 1, RUD 2, and ELEVATOR TRIM SHUTOFF (pilot/copilot) pushoff switchlights.

6. What advisory lights does the FCECU turn on?

The FCECU turns on the ROLL OUTBD and ROLL INBD advisory lights for the spoilers.
